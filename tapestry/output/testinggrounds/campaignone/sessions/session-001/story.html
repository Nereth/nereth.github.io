The Sigh That Forgot How to Finish

Lanterns floated like slow stars over the Upper District, their thin-paper skins trembling each time a chorus rose from the plaza. The city wore its joy proudly during the Starfall Convergence: silks and streamers and little silver bells, the air sweet with sugar and spiced milk, the sky a dark vault pricked with patient points of light. The broad stair of the plaza gleamed with swept stone, and the fountain at its heart threw silver into the night, sending a cool dampness toward flushed faces. Thalen watched from a stone balustrade, hood drawn, boots braced, the weight of his journal steady against one palm. He compared what the heavens offered to what he’d inked in solitude—arc paths, conjunctions, the obscene patience of stellar clocks. He could find comfort in a chart; it never lied. People did. And sometimes the sky itself forgot its promises.

Down in the crowd, Kivra fought a losing battle against abundance. She carried three skewers of fried dough, each dusted with shimmering sugar, a cup of something steaming, and a second cup tucked into the crook of her elbow, insurance against dryness or disappointment. She moved with the arrogant grace of someone who believed in snacks as both philosophy and shield. Heat from the fry-stalls licked her shins; cinnamon hung in the air with a promise of simple mercy. When she stopped near the fountain, crumbs glittered at her boots like tiny fallen stars.

Brother Moss stood by the fountain’s lip, hands folded, his eyes half-closed. He was large in a way that made people give him room, broad-shouldered and quiet, with the sort of stillness that pulled sound toward it. The water’s murmur had turned brittle. He listened to it the way a healer listens to a patient who has hidden pain too long, as though the fountain hid a bruise at its core.

“You keep saying that,” Kivra said, pinching off a crisp corner and offering it. “What does uneasy water sound like?”

“Like a sigh that forgot how to finish,” Brother Moss said. He did not take the pastry. “Like a thought interrupted.”

“Okay. Poetry, giant.” She kept the piece for herself.

Silver hair cut a neat line through the press of bodies as a woman in deep blue robes approached—sigils crawled along her sleeves in faint luminescence, and her expression had a distant, sleepless edge. She threaded through the festival as if gravity did the work for her, and when she reached the fountain she lifted a hand and somehow gathered them together without touching any of them, as if the city’s noise bent around her purpose.

“Thank you for meeting with me,” she said, when they’d found a pocket of space near the water. “I am Lyrian Vost.”

“Do you have fried doughnuts?” someone asked from behind Lyrian’s shoulder, bright and far too pleased. Speaker-00 sidled into the circle, her hair a mess of dark curls, her eyes with that glimmering, unruly focus of someone who read dreams like maps and maps like jokes.

Kivra lifted her sticks. “I take my snacks seriously.”

“This is wise,” Brother Moss agreed.

Lyrian did not smile. “The Astral Forge is destabilizing,” she said, and the easy brightness around them thinned. “The convergence tonight may worsen the effect. I need people who can act quickly if something goes wrong.”

“Define ‘something,’” Speaker-00 said, already leaning around Lyrian to stare at the sky.

“Rifts. Creatures. Distortions. Reality thinning.”

“Cool, cool, cool.”

Thalen’s hand tightened on his journal. Not a prophecy he liked.

“Because the Astral Forge whispered your names,” Lyrian added.

“Oh, that’s not ominous at all,” Speaker-00 said. She squinted up. “Fraying like a sweater, or fraying like reality exploding?”

Lyrian very slightly inclined her head. “The second one.”

“The sky cracks like glass,” Speaker-00 murmured, and then flung her arms out. “There it is.”

The first pulse of light was pure and silver, washing the plaza in a cold tide. Music clipped off mid-note. Cheers broke into shrieks. The air tore open above the farthest ring of lanterns, a slit in velvet widening until it showed nothing behind it at all. Three insect-things levered themselves through, legs like scissoring iron, faceted eyes milky and wide with unmeaning. They crawled down the empty air toward warm breath and soft skin and the terrible aesthetics of joy.

Kivra’s fried dough met the stones with a mortal patter. “R.I.P., dough,” she said. “A noble sacrifice.”

They moved together without looking to see that the others moved. The crowd surged away, wardens in cobalt sashes whistling and shoving, children scooped up and carried like parcels. Thalen was already striding, already drawing and setting. The first arrow went where he asked it to go—through an exposed seam, under a plate, into something that made the creature’s body shatter not like meat but like packed ash. It dissolved in a gray spray that hung glittering before gravity remembered its duty.

“You keep doing that,” Speaker-00 said, delighted. “Dusty bug!”

Sacred light kindled in Brother Moss’s upturned palm, not the zeal of a zealot but the precise mercy of someone who knew what needed to be done and did not confuse it with anger. He cast it toward the next creature as it scuttled over the fountain’s edge. The radiance took hold, and the thing let out a pitchless screech as plates warped and joints seized.

Kivra had both hands free now. She lifted her fingers and snapped them, the air heating of its own sudden will. Flame leapt like a fox and wrapped itself around chitin. Soot gusted.

Speaker-00 darted at the third insect, and for a heartbeat it looked like she might kick it over the edge of the plaza’s stone ring and down into the gardens below, sheer audacity making up the difference between poor plan and nimble aim. Her foot met carapace and slid. She pinwheeled a little, face twisted in mock betrayal. “My foot is also cursed,” she announced.

The creature pivoted toward Kivra and struck—plates slid, a limb scissored, and she yelped, stepping back with both hands up as if warding off a greasy insult. Sugar spattered from her sleeves.

Thalen did not aim for the center of mass that time. The second arrow threaded the quick, mean space between strike and recoil and anchored the thing to the fountain’s rim for the heartbeat it took for Brother Moss’s next light to find it. It exploded into a cloud of shimmering dust that caught in their hair and eyelashes.

Speaker-00 blew it from her lips. “We are unstoppable.”

“You almost died twice,” Brother Moss said mildly.

“I said what I said.”

Lyrian’s calm had a hairline crack in it. She glanced once to the ragged seam still faint in the sky and then back to them. “Thank the stars,” she breathed, and then, even as waves of panic rippled through the plaza, she fixed them with that same sleepless attention. “The Astral Forge is destabilizing. I need you to retrieve a focusing crystal from the lower vault.”

“So we break in,” Kivra said, already stooping with a small sound of mourning to assess the survivability of any remaining pastry.

“Yes, Kivra,” Lyrian said, as if she had correctly anticipated both remark and salvage attempt.

“What’s in the vault?” Speaker-00 asked, dream-bright.

“Artifacts. Dangerous ones.”

“Excellent.”

They went at once. Lyrian led them along the plaza’s edge, where spilled drink made the stones tacky and lanternlight trembled on the fountain’s skin. Wardens worked to pen the crowd; the four of them slid through the confusion under the shelter of Lyrian’s authority, through an arch of carved constellations into the observatory’s courtyard. The tower of the Astral Forge rose out of it—bronze-ribbed, slate-skinned, arched windows showing slivers of orreries that ticked mechanically against the night. The great doors admitted them with a dull sigh, muting the roar of festival into a low, persistent thrum. Inside smelled of hot metal and old parchment, of oil and ozone. Astrolabes hung like gilded skeletons from the vaulted ceiling, slow-moving, their brass arms catching lamplight in slow sweeps.

Down in the belly of the tower, all that celebration meant nothing. They took a spiral stair that wrapped the interior wall, torches guttering as though a tide had been running against them for hours. The stone underfoot was worn by generations of careful feet, and the air cooled with each step, taking on a clean hardness that sharpened the breath.

Something metallic scraped below. They paused on a landing, and Speaker-00 cupped her hands to her mouth and called, “Hello?”

“Why,” Thalen said dryly, “would you—”

“Stealth is for cowards,” Brother Moss said. His humor was dry as old leaves. He touched his sternum with two fingers and murmured a prayer that felt more like a promise he made to the space between his ribs. “I sense regret approaching.”

The antechamber’s brass door glowed faintly from within, the sigils along its face shifting like a patient eye. Lyrian’s key—cold metal, shaped like a star caught mid-fall—sat heavy in Speaker-00’s palm. She slid it into the lock with exaggerated delicacy and a flourish when it turned and the door eased.

“I did it,” she announced, almost offended by how little resistance it took.

Thalen stepped past her, deadpan. “It was a key.”

“I did it,” Speaker-00 repeated, louder.

Kivra bumped her with a hip as she went by. “Let her have this.”

“Fine,” Thalen said, and pretended not to have the corner of his mouth threaten to move.

The vault hall wore its danger openly. Shelves held objects wrapped and labeled in a careful, almost tender script; others sat naked on plinths with warning charms layered around them like cobwebs. One of them bled a slow coil of purple smoke from a crack in its surface, the strand pooling and then sifting toward the floor like bored incense. The air held a static feel, hairs on forearms lifting, breaths trying to become whispers of their own accord.

Speaker-00, predictably, reached. “Baby,” she whispered to the smoke, which did not answer.

“Stop touching things,” Thalen said, catching her wrist and drawing it back. The contact was brief. His hand was callused and warm, and the shock of being handled startled her into stillness.

“Fine.”

That’s when something metal rolled out from beneath a shelf, sprouting legs as it found them, a round body, a single lens burning in its face like a lit coal. It clicked once, focused, and charged.

“Baby,” Speaker-00 said, but Kivra already had her hands up, fire spilling through her fingers. The sphere tucked in its legs and hurled its weight at her; it caught her in the ribs and knocked the air from her with a sound like a deflating bellows. She hissed through her teeth, one arm thrown around her middle, eyes watering.

Brother Moss’s hand rose, and his flame fell with patient accuracy. The light took the sphere in the underside where gears showed. Its screech was all grinding metal and offended enchantment. Thalen’s arrow hit a fraction after, digging in and sticking, the shaft quivering as if its own balance were offended by such inelegant prey.

Kivra drew heat into her palms and threw it back. This time the fire did not splash but bit, wind-driven, opening the casing like a mouth. Something there did not enjoy oxygen. The explosion lifted all three of them back a step; heat snatched at eyebrows and fine hairs. Thalen slapped a smoldering scrap off his sleeve. Kivra blinked, eyes watering more now, and winced as the bruise at her ribs announced itself.

Speaker-00 flung her arms wide, laughing at the prickling skin on her cheeks. “Worth it?”

“You say that too often,” Thalen said, flicking ash from his cuff.

“And I mean it every time.”

A rumble ran through the floor under their boots, the stone muttering a warning. They didn’t linger. The inner door waited at the far end, its face carved into a serene sable visage with eyes closed. As they approached, they opened: stars picked out in the irises blinked polite inquiry. “Password?” it asked in a voice like a bell dropped in wool.

“No,” Speaker-00 said thoughtfully.

“Incorrect,” the door said, amused, “but poetic.”

The carved border around the face told a story: a hammer lifted beneath a spray of sparks, a forge mouth vomiting starfire, constellations smudged around the edges as though a careless artisan had thumbed at them to blur their hard lines. Thalen traced them with his gaze, memorizing. Old names whispered under his tongue without daring his breath. Speaker-00 bit her lip, cocked her head, and then said, “Gorin.”

The door smiled, soundless but understood, and it parted like lips.

“I am collecting this one,” Speaker-00 said to Thalen, and held up her palm. He watched it for a moment as if it were a trap, decided the trap would be too obvious, and let her slap his hand. It turned out to be just a high-five.

“This is growth,” Speaker-00 confided, winking at Kivra.

“Don’t make it weird.”

“Too late.”

The crystal sat on a low pedestal, humming in sympathy with the hair on their arms. Blue light lived in it, neither holy nor profane, only purposeful. Speaker-00 stepped forward, fingers stretched, then hesitated, glanced at Thalen, at Kivra, at Brother Moss, who were all doing varying degrees of necessary brace. She touched. The jolt leapt up her arm and through her shoulders, snapping her jaw shut on a curse that ended as a laugh.

“I regret nothing,” she said, shaking out her fingers. Sparks lingered between two rings.

Brother Moss set his palm gently against her forearm, and the ache unwound. He smelled like clean earth after rain. Kivra tugged a square of linen from her sash and Speaker-00 wrapped the crystal with quick, reverent clumsiness, as if tucking a restless child to bed.

“Thanks,” Speaker-00 said.

“I am not,” Brother Moss muttered, and she grinned at the lie.

The vault shuddered. That sleep-sick purple smoke uncoiled in a hurry, and the cracked object that had been bleeding it bulged. It broke along a seam and peeled like a fruit cut poorly, and what came out wore faces like clothing it took on and off between steps. It had too many corners where you only wanted curves and too many eyes where you wanted none, and it looked at them and then at the room and then at nothing, and all those choices seemed to hurt it.

Kivra lifted her palm and flung heat: the bolt streaked a clean line, kissing a face and leaving less than it found. Thalen dragged breath down his throat and drew—his first shot went wide as the thing turned all its attention at once toward him, and thoughts that were not his crawled into his mind and tried to rearrange it. His vision swam at the edges; the world took on a grainy, too-vivid clarity that made him want to rub his eyes until they bled. The creature’s lash was not claws but idea, and it hurt in ways his body hadn’t trained for. He heard Speaker-00’s soft “oof” as if she’d taken the bruise for him.

“Out,” Brother Moss said, and he flung his guiding light, but it skittered along the thing’s side like water on oiled glass, catching no purchase. His jaw set. He didn’t raise his voice when he missed; he simply chose differently and brought light down more humbly, a falling petal. It landed where arrogance failed.

Speaker-00 stepped to the side, head cocked to listen to something only she heard. “The stars approve,” she whispered. “So do the dreams. Also the floor. Hi, floor.”

Kivra’s next strike arced—not flame but a mean, quick lightning that snapped from her fingertip and sought the path of least resistance across the thing’s many faces. It convulsed and flickered and lost the one it had been wearing. Thalen steadied his hands and shot again. The arrow drove deep, and the wrongness that held the creature together failed with a sigh. Brother Moss’s falling light touched it once, and then it evaporated, like breath on glass, a ghost with the sense to leave.

Speaker-00 dusted off her sleeves. “We are unstoppable.”

“You almost died twice,” Brother Moss observed, which was generous; it had been at least that.

“I said what I said!”

The vault settled. The wrapped crystal in Speaker-00’s hands thrummed once more, the beat in it aligning with the tower’s bones. They did not speak much on the climb back up. Thalen listened to his breath and counted stairs, the stone cool under his palm; a slit window showed the lanterns still adrift, unbothered by what had crawled between their light. Kivra adjusted the knot of a sash to sit above the bruise blossoming at her ribs and kept her jaw tight each time the stair turned. Brother Moss walked last, making sure their shadows did not lag behind them. Speaker-00 murmured to the bundle she carried—flattery mostly, and promises it didn’t need from a mouth that liked the feel of promises anyway.

Lyrian was waiting when they emerged, relief an exhale that smoothed her mouth without softening her eyes. The engine hall of the Astral Forge opened around them—cavernous, all impossible geometry and humming purpose. Veins of light crawled through it in patient pathways, brass ribs arcing like the skeleton of a starship, crystal conduits threading from floor to ceiling. Earlier, the glow had sputtered and jumped; now it hung hesitant, waiting.

“Place it into the engine,” Lyrian said.

“I do it!” Speaker-00 announced, and slid the crystal into the cradle Lyrian indicated. It settled like it had always known where it belonged. The engine’s shivering calmed. The light ran through its channels without snagging now, a river remembering its bed. Heat rose in a clean draft; the hairs on their arms lay flat.

Silence fell inside the hall. Outside, through the slivered windows, the festival roared on, unaware and insistent.

“What now?” Thalen asked. He would mark the star paths later and see what had shifted while no one was looking. For now, he wanted a direction.

Lyrian’s mouth flattened into that sober line. “Now we find who is tampering with fate.”

“Forehead Doughnut Lady!” Speaker-00 said, bright and certain, pointing into some corner of memory. There had been a woman earlier with a sugared ring perched absurdly at her brow; there was always someone in a crowd who wore comedy like armor. “The dreams agree.”

“Of course they do,” Lyrian said, too tired to argue with dreams.

The engine’s pulse steadied under them. The city sang, and the water in the fountain resumed its breath, completing the sigh Brother Moss had heard. Outside, the lanterns went on floating, patient as stars. Inside, the names the Forge had whispered hung between them like a thread not yet pulled.
